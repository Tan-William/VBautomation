Demand Planning

The Importance of Forecast Accuracy
Wide forecast errors seem to happen every month - material and capacities are planned for one level of demand, but the actual demand that finds its way to the factory is something quite off the mark from the forecast. Whenever a forecast is created, it means there are also materials and components created, rescheduled or cancelled. If there is an increased forecast, chances are we will have overloaded schedule, plan over-time, add additional shift or new equipment, paying premium price to expedite raw material and incur expensive freight charges. When forecasted orders failed to happen, all these become added costs and unused inventories. Sales and Account teams giving overly optimistic forecast for fear of stockouts, but they need to understand the impact of excess inventory to the profitability of the company's business. The manager's job is to get all parties involved in the forecasting process to work as a team rather than finger-pointing and blaming, and take accountabilities for the forecast accuracy. When there is under-forecast and demand under-estimated, master scheduler and managers will become "traffic police", directing and coordinating to allocate company's limited output to certain customers and withholding it from others. Placing the customers on allocation gives a very bad reputation to the company.

Knowing that forecasts will never match demand, except on rare occasions or by luck, master scheduler must be experienced enough to shift material and capacity from one period to another, and know when to call the Sales team and customers so that it may be possible to split customer's delivery over two or more periods. Experienced master scheduler knows how to question those many hidden issues and problems in the forecast numbers, rather than just follow the forecast blindly and load the numbers into production plan.

 

Demand-Driven Planning Process

Demand can consist of a forecast, customer orders (which may or may not be part of that forecast), contract agreements, aftersale service spares, increased safety stock level and lot sizes, engineering prototypes, Distribution Center's replenishing orders, interplant orders, or orders from another division with the item ordered as a component of that division's products. The challenge the master scheduler faces is to effectively balance the supply resources with these demands. To satisfy these demands, we need to ensure availability of capacity and materials, with minimum qty, required dates and lead times all fully considered. With demand rises and falls unpredictably, we must also be quick to make necessary adjustment to supply resources (expedite/de-expedite PO delivery dates and qty, re-prioritizing start and completion dates of production orders, etc) in order to get supply and demand into balance.
